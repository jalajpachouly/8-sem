What is Sentiment Analysis?

Sentiment Analysis (also called opinion mining) is a technique in Natural Language Processing (NLP) that analyses text to determine the sentiment or emotional tone behind it — whether it is positive, negative, or neutral.

It is widely used in customer feedback analysis, social media monitoring, brand reputation tracking, and market research.

Types of Sentiment Analysis

1. Graded Sentiment Analysis

Goes beyond simple positive/negative. Assigns a degree of polarity to the sentiment across 5 levels.

"I absolutely love this!" → Very Positive
"The product is okay." → Neutral
"I hate this!" → Very Negative

Use case: interpreting 5-star ratings or surveys.

5-level polarity scale

2. Emotion Detection

Identifies specific emotions such as happiness, anger, sadness, or frustration using lexicons or ML models.

"I am so happy!" → Happiness
"This is frustrating!" → Frustration

Use case: customer service, mental health analysis.

Emotion wheel

3. Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis

Identifies sentiments related to specific aspects or features of a product or service rather than overall sentiment.

"Battery life is too short." → Negative (battery)
"Camera quality is amazing!" → Positive (camera)

Use case: product reviews, feature-level analysis.

Per-feature sentiment labels

4. Multilingual Sentiment Analysis

Analyses sentiments expressed in multiple languages. Requires language detection, translation/multilingual models, and language-specific lexicons.

"Este producto es increíble." (Spanish) → Positive

Use case: global businesses with multilingual feedback.

Sentiment across languages

5. Fine-Grained Sentiment Analysis

Provides detailed, fine-grained categories (Very Positive → Very Negative, often 5 classes like star ratings). Similar to graded but more structured.

"Extremely satisfied with service!" → Very Positive (5★)
"Service was okay, not great." → Neutral (3★)
5-star fine-grained scale

6. Intent-Based Sentiment Analysis

Focuses on identifying the intent behind the text — whether the user is interested, urgent, neutral, etc., rather than just polarity.

"I need this product immediately!" → Urgent
"I might consider buying this later." → Interested

Use case: marketing campaigns, customer service, lead generation.

Intent classification from text
